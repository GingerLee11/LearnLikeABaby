{% trans "Animelon" %}

{% blocktrans %} For all you anime enthusiasts out there (don't be shy, I know you're out there), Animelon is your go-to platform for learning Japanese through anime. {% trans "How to Use Animelon Effectively:" %}

{% blocktrans %} This advice applies to learners at all levels but is particularly beneficial for those at the Japanese Language Baby stage. Choose an anime that piques your interest, read its summary in your native language for context, and then watch it without subtitles. Trust me, it's a game-changer. Here's why this method works wonders. {% endblocktrans %}

{% blocktrans %} The goal isn't to understand every word but to familiarize your brain with the sound of the Japanese language. Your brain needs to recognize these sounds as meaningful, prompting it to allocate mental resources to understand them. But beware, our brains are inherently lazy! Using subtitles in your native language will only hinder this process. My advice? Go all in, but in short bursts to avoid mental fatigue. The Pomodoro Technique is your friend here. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Tofugu" %}

{% blocktrans %} Tofugu is a comprehensive resource that I've found invaluable in my own learning journey. While the site offers a plethora of information, my approach aims for rapid fluency with a native-like accent. I do NOT recommend learning Hiragana first, but I still think that Tofugu has the best guide to learning Hiragana so I will leave a link here. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "What's wrong with Hiragana?" %}

{% blocktrans %} I have nothing against Hiragana, and you may eventually wish that everything was written in Hiragana when you start takling Kanji (I'm not saying we should get rid of Kanji either, relax); however, I do advocate learning basic words through observation and listening before diving into writing and reading (Hiragana and Katakana AND Kanji) even if reading and writing are your preferred methods of language acquisition. Babies don't start by reading and writing, so why should language learners? Here's my detailed take on this. {% endblocktrans %}

日本語の森 - {% trans "Nihongonomori" %}

While not explicitly designed for non-native speakers, it aligns perfectly with my "learn like a baby" philosophy. {% trans "Podcasts for Learning Japanese" %}

{% blocktrans %} Podcasts offer a unique avenue for language acquisition, particularly in honing your listening skills. While traditional advice often suggests waiting until you reach an intermediate level to dive into podcasts, I advocate for incorporating them into your learning journey from day one. The absence of visual cues forces you to focus solely on auditory input, helping your brain adapt to the language's sounds and patterns. Initially, this might feel overwhelming, but the key is to let go of the need to understand every word. Instead, aim to familiarize yourself with the language's rhythm and intonation. To maximize the benefits, complement podcast listening with resources that offer visual context, gradually increasing your comprehension over time. I'll be elaborating on this approach in an upcoming blog post, so stay tuned! {% endblocktrans %}

歴史を面白く学ぶコテンラジオ (Coten Radio")

{% blocktrans %} Coten Radio is more than just a tool for learning Japanese; it's a captivating journey through history. The podcast features a diverse range of historical stories, presented in an engaging and accessible manner. Whether you're a history buff like me or new to the subject, you'll find the content surprisingly compelling. This isn't some obscure podcast either; it has received accolades such as the Japan Podcast Awards Grand Prize and the Spotify Prize, attesting to the quality and depth of its content. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Note:" %}

{% blocktrans %} The podcast is quite advanced, tackling topics that even native Japanese speakers might find challenging. However, one of the regular contributors is a self-proclaimed history "novice," ensuring that the discussions remain accessible. The hosts generally assume that listeners have little to no prior knowledge of the topics at hand, although they occasionally reference previous episodes for context. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "The Real Japanese Podcast by Haruka" %}

{% blocktrans %} Haruka's podcast offers a genuine glimpse into the Japanese language and culture. It's an excellent resource for learners who are looking for authentic, native-level content. What sets this podcast apart is Haruka's involvement in the language learning community; she even hosts meetups for Japanese learners in Japan, providing a unique opportunity for immersive learning. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Animes for Learning Japanese" %}

This list of anime has been curated not just based on personal preferences, but with a focus on their utility for Japanese language learners. These anime series offer a rich variety of content, from everyday conversations and specialized terms to cultural insights. They are all available for viewing on Animelon, which allows for free access and includes features to aid in language learning.

Anime List

うさぎドロップ (Usagi Drop)

Watch on Animelon
"Usagi Drop" is an anime that depicts family and parent-child relationships, featuring abundant everyday conversations and interactions within the household. As children are main characters, the Japanese language used is relatively easy to understand, with clear demonstrations of word meanings and usage. The anime also portrays scenes in the workplace, offering a dual opportunity to learn both everyday and professional Japanese.

三月のライオン (March Comes in Like a Lion)

Watch on Animelon
"March Comes in Like a Lion" deeply explores human relationships and emotional struggles. It's rich in expressions depicting emotions and states of mind, and also includes specialized terminology related to shogi (Japanese chess). It's particularly recommended for those interested in shogi, offering Japanese learners insights into emotional expressions and specialized vocabulary.

四月は君の嘘 (Your Lie in April)

Watch on Animelon
"Your Lie in April" is an anime themed around music and romance, portraying the touching story of its protagonists. It's filled with terms related to piano and classical music, making it a special recommendation for music lovers. For Japanese learners, it provides a wealth of emotional conversations and musical terms.

僕だけがいない街 (Erased)

Watch on Animelon
In "Erased," the protagonist travels back in time to solve a mystery, featuring dialects from Hokkaido and conversations where an adult's knowledge is expressed through a child's body. This offers Japanese learners a unique opportunity to experience both adult and child language usage, making it an excellent resource for learning diverse expressions.

ちはやふる (Chihayafuru)

Watch on Animelon
"Chihayafuru" revolves around competitive karuta (a card game based on Japanese poetry) and explores themes of motivation and aspiration. It's rich in specialized karuta terminology and expressions of youthful emotions, providing insights not only into language learning but also into Japanese culture and the sentiments of youth.

ラブ★コン (Lovely★Complex)

Watch on Animelon
"Love Com" is a romance-themed anime characterized by the use of Kansai dialect. It's ideal for learning everyday conversational Japanese related to romance, as well as getting a taste of regional language variations. It offers Japanese learners a chance to study diverse expressions used in romantic contexts and regional dialects.

からかい上手の高木さん (Teasing Master Takagi-san)

Watch on Animelon
This anime centers around school life and playful teasing, featuring abundant conversations and everyday school language. It allows viewers to experience aspects of Japanese student life and is suitable for learning natural conversation flows and school-related terms.

氷菓 (Hyouka)

Watch on Animelon
"Hyouka" is a mystery-themed anime with a strong emphasis on logical thinking and expression. The frequently repeated phrase "kininarimasu" (I'm curious) leaves a lasting impression, helping learners remember specific expressions. Although the vocabulary might be challenging, the repetitive situations make it easier to learn certain words and phrases.

坂本ですが？ (Haven't You Heard? I'm Sakamoto)

Watch on Animelon
"Haven't You Heard? I'm Sakamoto" is an anime with unique humor and characters. While its direct utility for Japanese learning might be debatable, its entertainment value is high, offering an enjoyable way to learn Japanese humor and figurative expressions.

ヲタクに恋は難しい (Wotakoi: Love is Hard for Otaku)

Watch on Animelon
"Wotakoi: Love is Hard for Otaku" depicts otaku culture and romance, featuring workplace conversations and otaku-specific language. It provides insights into contemporary Japanese office life and otaku culture, making it suitable for learners to study modern conversational Japanese and specialized terms.
